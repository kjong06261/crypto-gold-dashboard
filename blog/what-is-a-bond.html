What is a Bond?

Beginner Guide ‚Ä¢ 5 min read

A bond is essentially a loan that you make to a borrower‚Äîtypically a government or corporation. In exchange for lending your money, the borrower promises to pay you interest and return your principal at a specified future date. Bonds are one of the main asset classes alongside stocks and are often considered a more conservative investment.

1. How Bonds Work

When you buy a bond, you're lending money to the issuer. The issuer agrees to pay you a specified interest rate (called the coupon) and to repay the original amount (called the principal or face value) when the bond matures.

For example, if you buy a $1,000 bond with a 5% annual coupon that matures in 10 years, you would receive $50 per year in interest payments, and after 10 years, you'd get your $1,000 back.

üìã Key Terms

Face Value (Par): The amount the bond will be worth at maturity, typically $1,000

Coupon Rate: The annual interest rate paid on the bond

Maturity Date: When the bond expires and principal is repaid

Yield: The effective return on a bond, which can differ from the coupon rate

2. Types of Bonds

Government Bonds: Issued by national governments. U.S. Treasury bonds are considered among the safest investments because they're backed by the U.S. government. However, "safe" doesn't mean risk-free‚Äîthey can still lose value if interest rates rise.

Municipal Bonds: Issued by state and local governments to fund public projects. Interest is often exempt from federal taxes and sometimes state taxes.

Corporate Bonds: Issued by companies to raise capital. They typically offer higher interest rates than government bonds but carry more risk.

üí° The Risk-Return Tradeoff

Generally, bonds that offer higher interest rates carry more risk. A company offering a 10% yield is likely considered riskier than the U.S. government offering 4%. The higher rate compensates investors for taking on additional risk.

3. How Bond Prices Move

Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall. When interest rates fall, existing bond prices rise.

This happens because of competition. If you own a bond paying 3% and new bonds are issued paying 5%, your bond becomes less attractive. Its price must drop so that buyers get a competitive return.

4. Credit Risk

Credit risk is the possibility that the bond issuer won't be able to make interest payments or repay the principal. Rating agencies like Moody's, S&P, and Fitch assess this risk and assign credit ratings.

Bonds rated BBB- or higher are considered "investment grade." Bonds rated below this are called "high-yield" or "junk" bonds‚Äîthey offer higher interest rates but have a greater chance of default.

5. Risks of Bond Ownership

Many people think bonds are "safe," but they carry several risks:

Interest Rate Risk: If rates rise, your bond's market value falls. This matters if you need to sell before maturity.

Credit/Default Risk: The issuer might not pay. Even highly-rated issuers can run into trouble.

Inflation Risk: If inflation exceeds your bond's interest rate, your purchasing power declines even though you're receiving payments.

Reinvestment Risk: When your bond matures or pays interest, you might have to reinvest at lower rates.

Conclusion

Bonds are debt instruments that pay interest and return principal at maturity. They're often viewed as less volatile than stocks, but they're not risk-free. Interest rate changes, credit risk, and inflation can all affect bond returns. Understanding these basics is essential before considering bonds as part of any investment approach.
