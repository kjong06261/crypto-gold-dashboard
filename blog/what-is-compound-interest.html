What is Compound Interest?

Beginner Guide‚Ä¢5 min read

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. It's sometimes called "interest on interest" and is a fundamental concept in finance.

1. Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. If you invest $1,000 at 5% simple interest, you earn $50 per year, every year‚Äîalways based on the original $1,000.

Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus any accumulated interest. In year one, you earn $50 on $1,000. In year two, you earn interest on $1,050. The base keeps growing.

Compound Interest Formula
A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
A = Final amount | P = Principal | r = Interest rate | n = Compounding frequency | t = Time in years

2. The Power of Time

Compound interest has a more dramatic effect over longer periods. The longer money compounds, the faster it grows‚Äîthis is often called the "snowball effect."

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Reality Check

Compound interest calculations assume a consistent, positive return. Real investments don't work this way‚Äîreturns vary year to year and can be negative. These examples illustrate a mathematical concept, not guaranteed outcomes.

3. Compounding Frequency

How often interest compounds affects the final amount. Interest can compound annually, semi-annually, quarterly, monthly, or even daily.

More frequent compounding results in slightly higher returns, all else being equal. However, the difference is often small compared to other factors like the interest rate and time period.

4. The Rule of 72

The "Rule of 72" is a quick way to estimate how long it takes for money to double. Divide 72 by the interest rate to get the approximate number of years.

At 6% interest: 72 √∑ 6 = 12 years to double. At 8% interest: 72 √∑ 8 = 9 years to double. This is an approximation that works reasonably well for typical interest rates.

5. Compound Interest Works Both Ways

Compound interest can work against you when you're borrowing money. Credit card debt, for example, compounds‚Äîmeaning you can end up paying interest on previously accumulated interest, causing debt to grow rapidly if not paid off.

Conclusion

Compound interest is a mathematical concept where interest accumulates on both principal and previous interest. While it's a powerful concept for understanding long-term growth, real-world returns are variable and uncertain. Understanding compound interest helps with financial literacy, but it shouldn't be confused with guaranteed investment outcomes.
