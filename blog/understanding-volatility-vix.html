Understanding Volatility and the VIX

Intermediate Guide‚Ä¢5 min read

Volatility measures how much and how quickly prices change. In investing, it's often used as a proxy for risk‚Äîhigher volatility means prices are swinging more dramatically, in either direction.

1. What Volatility Means

A "volatile" stock might gain 5% one day and lose 4% the next. A "stable" stock might only move 0.5% in either direction. Volatility doesn't tell you which direction prices will move‚Äîjust how much they're likely to swing.

Mathematically, volatility is often measured as the standard deviation of returns over a period. Higher standard deviation = higher volatility.

2. The VIX Explained

The VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) measures the market's expectation of volatility in the S&P 500 over the next 30 days. It's calculated from the prices of S&P 500 options.

üìä VIX Levels (General Guidelines)

Below 15: Low volatility, often associated with complacent markets

15-20: Average volatility, typical market conditions

20-30: Elevated volatility, increased uncertainty

Above 30: High volatility, often during market stress or crises

*These are rough guidelines, not actionable trading signals.

3. Why VIX is Called the "Fear Index"

The VIX often rises when stock prices fall sharply because investors buy options for protection, which increases option prices and therefore the VIX. This correlation between market declines and VIX spikes led to its "fear gauge" nickname.

However, the VIX can also rise in flat or rising markets if uncertainty increases, so the "fear" label is somewhat oversimplified.

‚ö†Ô∏è VIX Products Are Not the VIX

ETFs and ETNs that claim to track the VIX actually track VIX futures, not the VIX itself. Due to contango (futures typically costing more than spot), these products often lose value over time even when the VIX is stable. They are not suitable for long-term holding and have caused significant losses for uninformed investors.

4. Volatility and Risk

In academic finance, volatility is often used as a measure of risk. However, this is controversial. Some argue that volatility isn't true risk‚Äîonly permanent loss of capital is. Stocks can be volatile but still generate strong long-term returns.

That said, high volatility can be psychologically challenging and may cause investors to make poor decisions, like selling at the bottom.

5. Using Volatility Information

For most individual investors, volatility information is more educational than actionable. Knowing that markets are volatile helps set expectations, but trying to trade based on volatility predictions is extremely difficult and risky.

Conclusion

Volatility measures how much prices fluctuate. The VIX measures expected volatility in the S&P 500 and tends to rise during market stress. While understanding volatility is valuable, attempting to trade it‚Äîespecially through VIX products‚Äîis complex and has caused significant losses for many investors.
