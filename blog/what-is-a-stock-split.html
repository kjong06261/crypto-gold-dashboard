What is a Stock Split?

Beginner Guide‚Ä¢4 min read

A stock split is when a company divides its existing shares into multiple shares. The total value of your holdings doesn't change‚Äîyou just own more shares at a proportionally lower price per share.

1. How Stock Splits Work

In a 2-for-1 stock split, each share becomes two shares, and the price per share is cut in half. If you owned 10 shares at $100 each ($1,000 total), after the split you'd own 20 shares at $50 each (still $1,000 total).

üìä Common Split Ratios

2-for-1: 1 share becomes 2; price halves

3-for-1: 1 share becomes 3; price divided by 3

4-for-1: 1 share becomes 4; price divided by 4

10-for-1: 1 share becomes 10; price divided by 10

2. Why Companies Split Stocks

Accessibility: A lower share price can make a stock more accessible to retail investors who might not want to (or be able to) buy a $500+ share.

Liquidity: More shares at a lower price can increase trading volume.

Perception: Some companies prefer their stock to trade in a certain price range.

Note: With fractional shares now available at many brokerages, the accessibility argument is less relevant than it once was.

üí° Key Point: No Value Created

A stock split doesn't create value. It's like exchanging a $20 bill for two $10 bills‚Äîyou have more pieces, but the same total value. A split alone is neither good nor bad news about the company.

3. Reverse Stock Splits

A reverse stock split is the opposite: multiple shares are combined into fewer shares at a higher price. In a 1-for-10 reverse split, 10 shares become 1 share at 10x the price.

Companies often do reverse splits to avoid delisting from exchanges that require minimum share prices, or to improve their image if the stock price has fallen very low. Reverse splits are sometimes viewed negatively because they often occur in struggling companies.

4. Impact on Options and Dividends

When a stock splits, options contracts are adjusted proportionally. Dividends per share are also adjusted‚Äîif you received $1 per share before a 2-for-1 split, you'd receive $0.50 per share after (but own twice as many shares, so total dividends unchanged).

Conclusion

Stock splits change the number of shares and price per share but don't change your total investment value or the company's fundamentals. They're largely cosmetic events. A split announcement shouldn't be a primary reason to buy or sell a stock.
