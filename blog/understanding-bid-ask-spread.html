Understanding Bid-Ask Spread

Intermediate Guide‚Ä¢5 min read

The bid-ask spread is the difference between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (bid) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (ask). It's a fundamental concept in trading that represents a real cost beyond commissions.

1. Bid and Ask Explained

Bid Price: The highest price buyers are currently offering to pay for a security. If you want to sell immediately, you'll receive the bid price.

Ask Price: The lowest price sellers are currently willing to accept. If you want to buy immediately, you'll pay the ask price.

The ask is always higher than the bid. This difference is the spread.

üìä Example

Bid: $99.50

Ask: $99.60

Spread: $0.10 (or 0.1%)

2. Why the Spread Matters

The spread is an implicit trading cost. If you buy at the ask ($99.60) and immediately sell at the bid ($99.50), you lose $0.10 per share‚Äîeven with zero commissions.

For active traders making many transactions, spreads can significantly impact returns. For long-term investors making infrequent trades, the impact is smaller but still worth understanding.

üìã What Affects Spread Size

Liquidity: Heavily traded stocks typically have tighter spreads

Volatility: Spreads often widen during market turbulence

Market Hours: Spreads may be wider in pre-market or after-hours trading

Security Type: Some assets (small stocks, some bonds) have naturally wider spreads

3. Spread as a Liquidity Indicator

A tight spread (small difference) generally indicates a liquid market with many buyers and sellers. This makes it easier to enter and exit positions without significant cost.

A wide spread suggests fewer market participants, making trading more expensive and potentially more difficult during market stress.

üí° Practical Tip

Using limit orders instead of market orders allows you to specify your price, potentially getting a better fill than the current bid or ask. However, limit orders aren't guaranteed to execute if the market doesn't reach your price.

4. Who Earns the Spread?

Market makers‚Äîfirms that continuously offer to buy and sell securities‚Äîearn the spread as compensation for providing liquidity and taking on inventory risk. They buy at the bid and sell at the ask, profiting from the difference.

Conclusion

The bid-ask spread is the difference between buying and selling prices, representing a real cost of trading. Tighter spreads indicate more liquid markets. Understanding spreads helps in recognizing the true cost of transactions beyond just commissions.
