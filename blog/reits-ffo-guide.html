Understanding FFO and AFFO in REIT Analysis

Concept Explanation ‚Ä¢ Jan 2026

When analyzing Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs), some analysts use different metrics than those typically applied to other stocks. This is partly because heavy depreciation charges on physical properties can make Net Income less useful for understanding cash generation. This article explains two commonly referenced metrics: FFO (Funds From Operations) and AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations).

1. What is FFO (Funds From Operations)?

FFO is a metric developed by Nareit (the National Association of Real Estate Investment Trusts) to measure REIT operating performance. It adds back depreciation and amortization to net income, based on the rationale that real estate may not lose value in the way that depreciation accounting suggests.

Basic FFO Calculation
Net Income + Depreciation + Amortization ‚àí Gains on Sales of Property = FFO

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Limitation

FFO is a non-GAAP metric, meaning companies have some flexibility in how they calculate it. Comparisons between REITs should account for potential differences in methodology.

2. What is AFFO (Adjusted FFO)?

AFFO attempts to go further than FFO by subtracting recurring capital expenditures needed to maintain properties (such as roof repairs, HVAC replacement, etc.). Some analysts view AFFO as a closer approximation of sustainable cash flow available for dividends.

Basic AFFO Calculation
FFO ‚àí Recurring Capital Expenditures ‚àí Straight-line Rent Adjustments = AFFO

‚ö†Ô∏è Calculation Varies

There is no standardized definition of AFFO. Different REITs calculate it differently, making direct comparisons challenging. Some companies may use terms like "CAD" (Cash Available for Distribution) or "FAD" (Funds Available for Distribution) with different adjustments.

3. Payout Ratio Considerations

When analyzing REIT dividends, some investors look at payout ratios based on FFO or AFFO rather than Net Income. A payout ratio measures what percentage of a metric is paid out as dividends.

However, using any single metric as a "safe" threshold for dividend sustainability is an oversimplification. Dividend sustainability depends on many factors including tenant quality, debt levels, interest rate environment, and overall business strategy‚Äînot just a single ratio.

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations of FFO and AFFO

  • Non-Standardized: These are non-GAAP metrics with varying calculation methods across companies
  • Backward-Looking: Historical FFO/AFFO doesn't predict future performance
  • Manipulation Risk: Companies have discretion in certain adjustments
  • Not a Guarantee: Strong FFO/AFFO metrics don't guarantee dividend safety or stock price stability
  • Ignores Other Risks: These metrics don't capture interest rate risk, tenant credit risk, or sector disruption
  • Capital Structure Blind: FFO/AFFO don't directly reflect leverage or refinancing risks

4. Using These Metrics in Context

FFO and AFFO can be useful as part of a broader analysis, but they should not be relied upon in isolation. Other factors to consider include: the REIT's debt maturity schedule, tenant concentration, lease expiration timing, interest rate sensitivity, and management track record.

No single metric or set of metrics can predict whether a REIT investment will be successful. These tools provide one lens for understanding REIT operations but have significant limitations.

Conclusion

FFO and AFFO are commonly referenced metrics in REIT analysis that attempt to better capture cash generation than traditional Net Income. However, they are non-standardized, have calculation variations, and should be used alongside other analysis methods. Understanding these terms can be helpful for reading REIT financial reports, but familiarity with the terminology does not reduce the inherent risks of REIT investing.
