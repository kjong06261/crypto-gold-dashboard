FFO vs AFFO: The Pulse of REIT Investing

Equity Research Team ‚Ä¢ Jan 6, 2026

When evaluating traditional stocks, the P/E ratio is king. But for Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs), using Net Income can be a fatal mistake. Due to heavy depreciation charges on physical properties, a REIT might appear to be losing money on paper while generating massive amounts of cash. To see the truth, we must use FFO (Funds From Operations) and AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations).

1. What is FFO (Funds From Operations)?

FFO is the standard industry metric used to measure the operating performance of a REIT. It adds back non-cash items like depreciation and amortization to net income, as real estate typically appreciates over time rather than losing value like a piece of machinery.

FFO Calculation
Net Income + Depreciation + Amortization - Gains on Sales of Property = FFO

2. AFFO: The Gold Standard of Dividend Safety

While FFO is great, AFFO goes a step further. It subtracts recurring capital expenditures (CAPEX) required to maintain the properties (like roof repairs or carpet replacement). AFFO represents the actual "discretionary" cash left over to pay dividends.

AFFO Calculation
FFO - Recurring CAPEX - Straight-line Rent Adjustments = AFFO

3. Why the Payout Ratio Matters

Never use a REIT's payout ratio based on Net Income. Always look for the AFFO Payout Ratio. A healthy REIT typically keeps this between 70% and 85%. If it exceeds 95%, the dividend is at risk if a major tenant leaves or interest rates spike.
