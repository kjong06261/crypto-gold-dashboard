What are Treasury Bonds?

Intermediate Guide‚Ä¢5 min read

Treasury securities are debt instruments issued by the U.S. government to finance its operations. When you buy a Treasury, you're lending money to the federal government, which promises to repay with interest. They're often considered among the lowest-risk investments available.

1. Types of Treasury Securities

üìä Treasury Security Types

Treasury Bills (T-Bills): Short-term, maturing in 4 weeks to 1 year. Sold at a discount; you receive face value at maturity.

Treasury Notes (T-Notes): Medium-term, maturing in 2-10 years. Pay interest every 6 months.

Treasury Bonds (T-Bonds): Long-term, maturing in 20-30 years. Pay interest every 6 months.

TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities): Principal adjusts with inflation (CPI). Available in 5, 10, and 30-year terms.

I Bonds: Savings bonds with rates tied to inflation. Subject to purchase limits.

2. Why Treasuries are Considered "Safe"

U.S. Treasuries are backed by the "full faith and credit" of the federal government. The U.S. has never defaulted on its debt obligations, and the government can raise taxes or, theoretically, create money to meet its obligations.

This doesn't mean Treasuries are risk-free‚Äîthey carry interest rate risk and inflation risk‚Äîbut credit risk (the risk of not being repaid) is considered minimal.

‚ö†Ô∏è "Safe" Doesn't Mean "Risk-Free"

If you sell a Treasury before maturity, you may receive less than you paid if interest rates have risen. And if inflation exceeds your Treasury's yield, your purchasing power decreases. Treasuries guarantee repayment of principal at maturity, not positive real returns.

3. How to Buy Treasuries

TreasuryDirect: The government's website where you can buy Treasuries directly without fees.

Brokerage Accounts: Most brokerages allow purchasing Treasuries on the secondary market.

Treasury ETFs/Funds: Funds that hold baskets of Treasuries, providing diversification and liquidity.

4. The Yield Curve

The yield curve plots Treasury yields across different maturities. Normally, longer-term Treasuries yield more than shorter-term ones to compensate for the longer commitment.

When short-term yields exceed long-term yields, it's called an "inverted yield curve"‚Äîhistorically associated with economic recessions, though the relationship isn't guaranteed.

5. Risks of Treasury Investing

Interest Rate Risk: If rates rise, the market value of your Treasuries falls. This matters if you sell before maturity.

Inflation Risk: Fixed payments may lose purchasing power if inflation exceeds expectations.

Opportunity Cost: Lower yields mean potentially lower returns compared to riskier investments.

Reinvestment Risk: When your Treasury matures, you may have to reinvest at lower rates.

Conclusion

Treasury securities are government-issued debt instruments often used for capital preservation. While they carry minimal credit risk, they're still subject to interest rate and inflation risks. Understanding the different types and their characteristics can help in evaluating whether they fit your financial situation.
