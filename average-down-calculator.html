Averaging down is NOT always a good strategy. Consider these risks:

‚Ä¢ You're investing more money into a losing position

‚Ä¢ The stock could continue to decline, increasing your losses

üìä How Averaging Down Works

Averaging down means buying more shares at a lower price to reduce your average cost per share.

Formula: New Average = (Original Cost + Additional Cost) √∑ Total Shares

Example: You own 100 shares at $50 ($5,000). You buy 50 more at $40 ($2,000). New average = $7,000 √∑ 150 = $46.67

‚úÖ When Averaging Down Makes Sense

‚Ä¢ Temporary Setback: Stock dropped due to short-term bad news, not fundamental problems

‚Ä¢ Market Overreaction: Price decline is not justified by company performance

‚Ä¢ Strong Fundamentals: Company still has solid earnings, revenue growth, and business model

‚Ä¢ Long-term Investment: You're investing for years, not trading short-term

‚Ä¢ Available Capital: You have extra money you can afford to lose

‚ùå When NOT to Average Down

‚Ä¢ Fundamental Issues: Company has serious business problems or declining market share

‚Ä¢ Broken Thesis: The reason you bought the stock is no longer valid

‚Ä¢ Catching a Falling Knife: Stock is in free fall with no support level

‚Ä¢ Limited Capital: You're using money you can't afford to lose

‚Ä¢ Revenge Trading: You're emotionally trying to "get even" with the market

üí° Smarter Alternatives

‚Ä¢ Cut Your Losses: Accept the loss and move on (sometimes the best decision)

‚Ä¢ Dollar-Cost Averaging: Buy fixed amounts at regular intervals instead of trying to time dips

‚Ä¢ Diversify Instead: Use the money to buy a different stock and reduce concentration risk

‚Ä¢ Wait for Confirmation: Let the stock show signs of recovery before adding more
