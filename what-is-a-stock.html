What is a Stock?

Beginner Guide ‚Ä¢ 5 min read

A stock represents a share of ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a partial owner‚Äîcalled a shareholder‚Äîof that business. This is one of the most fundamental concepts in investing, yet it's often misunderstood by beginners.

1. How Stocks Work

Companies issue stocks to raise money. Instead of borrowing from a bank, a company can sell small pieces of ownership to the public. In exchange for your money, you receive shares that represent your ownership stake.

For example, if a company has 1,000,000 shares outstanding and you own 1,000 shares, you own 0.1% of that company. As a shareholder, you typically have certain rights, such as voting on major company decisions and receiving dividends if the company distributes profits.

üìã Key Terms

Share: A single unit of ownership in a company

Shareholder: A person or entity that owns shares in a company

Outstanding Shares: The total number of shares that exist for a company

Equity: Another word for stock or ownership

2. How Stock Prices Move

Stock prices change based on supply and demand. If more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the price tends to go up. If more people want to sell than buy, the price tends to go down.

Many factors influence this supply and demand: company earnings, economic conditions, industry trends, investor sentiment, and countless other variables. This is why predicting stock prices is extremely difficult‚Äîeven for professionals.

üí° Important to Understand

Stock prices can be volatile, meaning they can change significantly in short periods. A stock's price going up or down doesn't necessarily reflect the company's actual value‚Äîit reflects what buyers and sellers think it's worth at that moment.

3. Types of Stock

There are two main types of stock that companies issue:

Common Stock is the most typical form. Common shareholders can vote on company matters and may receive dividends, but they're last in line if the company goes bankrupt.

Preferred Stock is a hybrid between stocks and bonds. Preferred shareholders typically receive fixed dividends and have priority over common shareholders if the company liquidates, but usually don't have voting rights.

4. How to Buy Stocks

Stocks are bought and sold on stock exchanges, such as the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ. Individual investors typically access these exchanges through brokerage accounts.

When you place an order to buy a stock, you're matched with someone willing to sell at that price. The transaction happens almost instantly during market hours.

5. Risks of Stock Ownership

Owning stocks comes with significant risks that every investor should understand:

Market Risk: Stock prices can decline due to overall market conditions, even if the company itself is performing well.

Company Risk: A specific company can underperform, face financial difficulties, or even go bankrupt‚Äîpotentially making your shares worthless.

Volatility Risk: Stock prices can swing dramatically in short periods, which can be stressful and may lead to poor decision-making.

No Guaranteed Returns: Unlike savings accounts or bonds, stocks don't promise any return. You could lose some or all of your investment.

Conclusion

Stocks represent ownership in companies and are one of the primary ways people invest. Understanding what stocks are is just the first step‚Äîthere's much more to learn about how to evaluate companies, build portfolios, and manage risk. Remember that all stock investments carry the potential for loss, and past performance never guarantees future results.
